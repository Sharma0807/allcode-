History Of Bihar

The history of Bihar is ancient and glorious. In the Vedic period, it was known for powerful regions like Videha, Magadha, and Anga. By the 6th century BCE, Magadha had emerged as a dominant Mahajanapada, with its capitals at Rajgir and later Pataliputra (modern-day Patna). Bihar is the land where Gautama Buddha attained enlightenment and where Lord Mahavira, the 24th Tirthankara of Jainism, was born—making it a significant center for both Buddhism and Jainism. The Maurya Empire, founded by Chandragupta Maurya, expanded from this region, and his grandson Emperor Ashoka spread Buddhism far and wide from here. During the Gupta period, often called the “Golden Age of India,” Bihar became a hub of education and culture, with the establishment of world-renowned universities like Nalanda and Vikramshila. In the medieval era, the Pala dynasty promoted Buddhism, but with the arrival of Muslim invaders like Bakhtiyar Khilji, many Buddhist institutions including Nalanda were destroyed. Under the Mughal Empire, Bihar was an important administrative region. After the Battle of Buxar in 1764, it came under British East India Company rule. During the 1857 revolt, Veer Kunwar Singh of Bihar played a heroic role against the British. In 1912, Bihar was separated from Bengal to become a separate province. In 1917, Mahatma Gandhi launched the Champaran Satyagraha in Bihar, his first civil disobedience movement in India. With India's independence in 1947, Bihar became part of the Republic of India, and in 2000, the southern part of Bihar was carved out to form the state of Jharkhand. The name "Bihar" originates from the Sanskrit word Vihara, meaning monastery, as the region was full of Buddhist monasteries. Hence, Bihar was never "discovered" at a specific time—it has been known and culturally significant since ancient times. Today, Bihar remains an important state in terms of history, culture, and politics, and continues to progress toward development.

Nalanda

nalanda University is historically significant. Nalanda University, located in the modern-day Bihar state of India, was one of the most prominent centers for the study of Buddhism in ancient times. It was founded in the 5th century CE during the reign of Kumaragupta I of the Gupta Empire. After the teachings of Gautama Buddha (around the 5th century BCE) spread, Nalanda became a major educational institution for the study of Buddhist philosophy and scriptures. It played a pivotal role in the development and dissemination of Buddhist thought.At its peak in the 7th century CE, Nalanda was a leading center for Buddhist learning, where scholars from all over Asia came to study. The famous Chinese traveler and scholar Xuanzang (Hiuen Tsang) visited Nalanda between 629 CE and 645 CE and spent several years studying Buddhism and Buddhist philosophy. He praised the institution for its rich knowledge and peaceful academic environment. Prominent Buddhist scholars such as Aryadeva and Dipankara Shrijnana taught at Nalanda, contributing to the spread and deepening of Buddhist knowledge.The university housed an enormous library with hundreds of thousands of manuscripts, many of which were dedicated to Buddhist texts. These texts covered a wide range of topics, from Buddhist teachings to advanced philosophy and sciences.However, Nalanda University met its downfall in the 12th century CE, when it was destroyed by Bakhtiyar Khilji, a Turkish military general, around 1193 CE. The invasion led to the burning of its great library and the destruction of this center of Buddhist scholarship. Despite this, Nalanda's historical significance in the context of Buddhism remains immense, and the site today is recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site (since 2016).In summary, Nalanda University and Buddhism were closely intertwined, with the university serving as a major center for Buddhist study and intellectual exchange for many centuries. Its contributions to Buddhist learning and the spread of Buddhism throughout Asia are invaluable.

Bodh Gaya

Bodh Gaya, located in the state of Bihar, India, is one of the most significant and revered places in the history of Buddhism. It is the site where Gautama Buddha (Siddhartha Gautama) attained enlightenment (Bodhi) under the Bodhi tree, making it the most sacred Buddhist pilgrimage site. According to tradition, around 528 BCE, Gautama Buddha, after a long period of meditation and self-reflection, achieved enlightenment at this spot while meditating beneath the Bodhi tree. This moment marked the beginning of the spread of Buddhist teachings across the world.Historically, Bodh Gaya has been a place of spiritual significance for centuries. The first major monument at Bodh Gaya was built by the Mauryan Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BCE, who visited the site after converting to Buddhism. Ashoka erected a pillar at the site, marking it as a place of great importance. Later, Gupta rulers in the 5th century CE and other Buddhist patrons contributed to the further development of the area, building temples and monasteries.The Bodhi tree under which Buddha attained enlightenment has had several generations of trees planted after the original tree died. The current tree is said to be a descendant of the original tree, and it remains an integral part of the site. Bodh Gaya also features the Mahabodhi Temple, which was constructed in the 6th century CE and is a major pilgrimage site for Buddhists worldwide. Bodh Gaya's significance continued to grow over the centuries, and in 2002, the Mahabodhi Temple was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognizing its outstanding universal value as a center of Buddhism. Today, Bodh Gaya continues to draw millions of visitors and pilgrims from all over the world who come to pay homage to the place where Buddha achieved his spiritual enlightenment.




Rajgir

Rajgir located in the Nalanda district of Bihar, India, is a place of immense historical, religious, and cultural significance, particularly in the context of Buddhism and Jainism. The ancient city, known as Rajagriha in ancient times, served as the capital of the Magadh Kingdom under the rule of King Bimbisara of the Haryanka dynasty (around 6th century BCE). It was later also the capital during the reign of Ajatashatru, Bimbisara's son. Rajgir is particularly famous for its association with Gautama Buddha, who visited this city numerous times during his lifetime. It is here that the First Buddhist Council was held around 483 BCE after the death of Buddha. This council, presided over by Mahakasyapa, was convened to preserve and codify Buddha's teachings (the Sutras) and the monastic discipline (the Vinaya).The city also holds significance in the Jain tradition. It is believed that Lord Mahavira, the 24th Tirthankara, lived and preached in Rajgir for many years, and it is here that he delivered several of his important sermons. A prominent place associated with Mahavira in Rajgir is the Griddhakuta Hill, also known as Vultures Peak, where he is said to have given many teachings.Rajgir was also famous for its natural beauty and was surrounded by hills, making it a retreat for many religious figures. The hot springs in the region were believed to have healing properties, and it is said that King Bimbisara used them for medicinal purposes.Over the centuries, Rajgir lost some of its prominence but remained an important religious and cultural site. Today, it is a significant pilgrimage destination for Buddhists, Jains, and Hindus. The Brahmakund hot springs, the Vultures Peak (Griddhakuta), and the Jivaka's Mango Garden are notable attractions in Rajgir. Additionally, the Shanti Stupa (Peace Pagoda) built on the hilltop in 1969 by the Japanese Buddhist organization is an important landmark.In 2002, the Rajgir Glass Bridge was constructed, further enhancing the site's importance as a tourist and pilgrimage destination. The Buddhist relics and ruins around Rajgir continue to draw people interested in the region's rich cultural and religious history.




Lauriya Nandangarh (West Champaran)

Lauriya Nandangarh, located in the West Champaran district of Bihar, is an important historical and archaeological site, primarily known for its association with Emperor Ashoka and the Mauryan Empire. The town gets its name from a pillar (Lauriya refers to Lāt, meaning pillar in Hindi) that stands here — one of the well-preserved Ashokan pillars dating back to the 3rd century BCE. This polished sandstone pillar, about 10.7 meters high, bears the Edicts of Ashoka, which promote moral living, non-violence, and the principles of Dhamma (Dharma). It is believed that Emperor Ashoka erected this pillar after converting to Buddhism, as part of his effort to spread Buddhist philosophy across his empire. Near the pillar, Nandangarh is home to a group of large Buddhist stupas, believed to have been built between the 3rd century BCE and 1st century CE, possibly to enshrine the relics of the Buddha or important Buddhist monks. These stupas, arranged geometrically, reflect the area's significance as a major Buddhist religious and cultural center during the Mauryan and post-Mauryan periods. Today, Lauriya Nandangarh stands as a testimony to Bihar's ancient Buddhist heritage and Ashoka's role in spreading peace, compassion, and Buddhist teachings throughout the Indian subcontinent.





Pataliputra

Pataliputra known today as Patna, is one of the most ancient and historically significant cities of India. It was originally founded in the 5th century BCE by Ajatashatru, a ruler of the Haryanka dynasty, who shifted his capital from Rajgir to this strategically located site at the confluence of the Ganga, Gandak, and Sone rivers. Over centuries, Pataliputra grew into a major political, cultural, and commercial hub and served as the imperial capital for several great Indian dynasties, including the Maurya, Shunga, Kanva, Gupta, and Pala empires. Under Chandragupta Maurya and later his grandson Ashoka the Great in the 3rd century BCE, Pataliputra reached the height of its glory, becoming the center of governance and the global heart of Buddhist dissemination. The Greek ambassador Megasthenes, who visited during Chandragupta's reign, described the city's grandeur and well-organized administration in his famous work Indica. During the Gupta period (4th-6th century CE), Pataliputra continued to flourish as a hub of learning, science, and art. However, over time, due to repeated invasions and natural calamities, the city's political importance declined. It was later revived as Patna and became an important center during the Mughal and British periods, especially as a trading city. Today, Patna stands as the modern capital of Bihar and is considered one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world, preserving the rich legacy of ancient Pataliputra, once the crown jewel of Indian civilization.



Vikramshila (Bhagalpur)

Vikramshila University was one of the most important centers of Buddhist learning in ancient India, established in the late 8th century CE by Emperor Dharmapala of the Pala dynasty (who ruled around 770-810 CE). It was located in the present-day Bhagalpur district of Bihar, near the banks of the Ganga River. Vikramshila was founded as a response to a perceived decline in the quality of education at Nalanda University and was intended to become a new hub for advanced Buddhist studies, particularly for Vajrayana (Tantric Buddhism). The university quickly gained prominence, housing more than 1,000 students and around 100 scholars, with a vast complex that included a central monastery (Mahavihara), lecture halls, libraries, meditation halls, and beautifully constructed gateways.One of its most renowned scholars was Atisha Dipankara Shrijnana, who later played a key role in the revival of Buddhism in Tibet during the 11th century. Vikramshila maintained a rigorous academic structure and strict discipline, with a competitive entrance system. It became a center of scholarly excellence, not only in Buddhist philosophy but also in grammar, logic, metaphysics, and other subjects. Unfortunately, like Nalanda, Vikramshila was destroyed in the 12th century CE, around 1193, during the invasions of Bakhtiyar Khilji, a Turkish general, leading to the collapse of one of the last great Buddhist institutions in India.



Kundalpur (Nalanda)

Kundalpur located near Nalanda in the Nalanda district of Bihar, holds significant religious and historical importance, particularly in relation to Jainism and Buddhism. It is traditionally believed to be the birthplace of Lord Mahavira, the 24th Tirthankara of Jainism, although some traditions also claim Vaishali as his birthplace. According to Jain texts, Mahavira was born in a royal family in Kundalpur around 599 BCE, and he spent much of his early life here before renouncing worldly life at the age of 30 in search of spiritual truth. As such, Kundalpur is a sacred site for Jain pilgrims and houses several ancient Jain temples and structures, many of which have been renovated over time. In addition to its Jain heritage, Kundalpur is also located close to Nalanda, the site of the ancient Nalanda University, and thus shares historical connections with Buddhism as well. The area around Kundalpur was an active zone of learning, spiritual dialogue, and pilgrimage during the time of both Mahavira and Buddha, who were contemporaries. Due to this dual religious significance, Kundalpur today attracts both Jain and Buddhist followers. Archaeological remains, ancient idols, and stone inscriptions found in and around Kundalpur highlight its role as a religious and cultural center in ancient Magadha.

Sitamarhi

Sitamarhi located in the northern part of Bihar, is a historically and religiously significant town in India, especially revered in Hindu mythology as the birthplace of Goddess Sita, the consort of Lord Rama from the epic Ramayana. According to legend, King Janaka, the ruler of the ancient kingdom of Mithila, discovered Sita while ploughing the fields during a severe famine as part of a yajna (ritual) to please the gods. She was found in a furrow (Sita) of the earth, which is why she was named Sita, and the place came to be known as Sitamarhi "the place of Sita." This makes Sitamarhi a sacred site for Hindus, and the Janaki Mandir located here is an important pilgrimage center. It is also closely connected with Janakpur in present-day Nepal, where Sita is believed to have grown up and later married Lord Rama.Historically, Sitamarhi has been a part of the ancient Mithila region, which was known for its rich Vedic culture, learning, and traditions. The area was a part of the Videha Kingdom, one of the prominent political and cultural centers during the Vedic period (roughly 1200-600 BCE). Sitamarhi has also seen the influence of Buddhism and Jainism, as both religions flourished in the greater Mithila and Bihar region during the time of Gautama Buddha and Mahavira. In modern history, Sitamarhi played a role during the freedom struggle of India and has grown into a vibrant district with cultural, agricultural, and religious importance.
